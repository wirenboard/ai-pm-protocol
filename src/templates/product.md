# Product brief

> The one home for **what this project is and why** — filled at onboarding (the orchestrator's `## Product discovery`), kept current; every feature grounds in it. Discovery deletes the first line's template marker when it fills this file — the marker is how the protocol tells an installed skeleton from a real brief.
>
> Filled gather-first, conclude-last: record what the Operator gives, mark the rest `[?]` — never invent an answer, never grade one. The case against (§7) comes at the end. Plain product language.

## 0. The idea — what is this product?

`<one plain sentence: what this product is and what it does. For a LEGACY product largely given — read it from the codebase; for a NEW one, ask. No scope-gate theater: arriving at this protocol already implies a software-or-documentation product.>`

> The **anchor for everything below.** Every question reasons AROUND this idea — its options stay plausible *given* the product (for an AI software/docs protocol: a PM, developer, founder, student — not an auto-mechanic). A question disconnected from the idea is a dead question.

## 1. Customer — who exactly?

`<the user(s) this is for — a role + the situation they are in when they reach for it. May be a spectrum (several roles the product genuinely serves), not forced to one — but name who it is plainly NOT for. "Everyone" is not an answer.>`

## 2. Problem — from their point of view

`<the real pain, or the job they hire this to do — in the customer's words, not ours. Not the solution.>`

## 3. Discovery & onboarding — zero to working

`<how does a new user FIND OUT this product exists? And the first steps from nothing to a working state — the zero-to-working story. If getting started needs more than the user alone (an install, an admin, a key, a prerequisite), name it.>`

## 4. Continuity & recovery

`<how does use carry across devices or sessions? What happens when a user loses access — a key, a device, their data? If others are involved, how do they join? A question genuinely N/A here is answered "N/A — <why>" (a recorded answer), never silently skipped.>`

## 5. Competition / the incumbent

`<what the customer uses TODAY to solve this — the tool, hack, workaround, or doing-nothing — and why this is MEANINGFULLY different. If the others are unknown, RESEARCH it first (the research side-tool: "how do others do this, and do they even exist?") before writing this. Solving it the same way as what exists ⇒ back to the drawing board.>`

## 6. Viability — who runs and funds it

`<who operates it, who funds it, and what legal or operational constraints bind it (licensing, privacy, compliance, cost). The bet has to survive contact with the real world. Use [?] for anything not yet fixed.>`

## 7. The case against *(the conclusion — at the end)*

`<having gathered all the above: the strongest reasons this will NOT succeed, who it is wrong for, and — honestly — whether it even fits what is being built. Licensed and required to conclude "the answers don't match what we built — the build is what's wrong." If you cannot name any real reason against, the discovery was a rubber stamp.>`
